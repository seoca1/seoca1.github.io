## 📌 한국어 학습자를 위한 요약

- **핵심:** "좋아하다" 를 표현하지만 영어/한국어와 다른 **역구조** 사용. "Me gusta el café" = "커피가 [나에게] 좋다".
- **구조:** 간접보어 (me/te/le/nos/les) + gusta/gustan + 주어
- **gusta vs gustan:** 단수 주어 → gusta, 복수 주어 → gustan
- **유사 동사:** encantar (매우 좋아하다), molestar (귀찮다), interesar (관심있다), importar (중요하다) — 같은 구조
- **활용:** [[Restaurant_Ordering]] 레슨에서 "Me gusta la paella" 처럼 메뉴 선호 표현에 사용
- **한국어 학습자 주의:** "I like coffee" 가 아니라 "Coffee is pleasing to me" 로 사고의 전환 필요

## 🇺🇸 Full English Content

# Gustar Verb (Verbo Gustar)



## What Makes Gustar Special?

**Gustar** means "to please" rather than "to like." Instead of the subject performing an action, the *thing being liked* is the subject, and the *liker* appears as an **indirect object pronoun**.

This inversion is one of the most distinctive features of Spanish and applies to several other verbs (encantar, molestar, etc.).

| English | Spanish structure |
|:---|:---|
| I like coffee. | **Coffee pleases to-me.** → *Me gusta el café.* |
| We like the books. | **The books please to-us.** → *Nos gustan los libros.* |

---

## The Core Formula

```
indirect object pronoun + gusta/gustan + subject
```

The verb form (**gusta** vs **gustan**) agrees with the **subject** (what is liked), not with the person who likes it.

---

## The 6 Indirect Object Pronouns

| Person | Pronoun | Example |
|:---:|:---:|:---|
| 1st sg. | **me** | to me |
| 2nd sg. | **te** | to you (tú) |
| 3rd sg. | **le** | to him/her/you (Ud.) |
| 1st pl. | **nos** | to us |
| 2nd pl. | **os** | to you all (vosotros) |
| 3rd pl. | **les** | to them/you all (Uds.) |

---

## gusta vs. gustan — When to Use Which

| Subject | Verb form | Reason |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Singular noun | **gusta** | singular subject → 3rd person singular |
| Plural noun | **gustan** | plural subject → 3rd person plural |
| Infinitive | **gusta** | infinitive is treated as singular |
| Multiple infinitives | **gustan** | multiple actions → plural |

### Examples

> *Me **gusta** el café.* (I like coffee. — singular noun)
> *Me **gustan** los libros.* (I like the books. — plural noun)
> *Me **gusta** leer.* (I like to read. — infinitive)
> *Me **gustan** leer y escribir.* (I like reading and writing. — multiple infinitives)

---

## Clarifying the Subject with "A + pronoun"

To avoid ambiguity with **le** and **les** (he/she/they/you formal), Spanish often adds a clarifying phrase:

> *A María **le** gusta el café.* (María likes coffee.)
> ***Le** gusta el café a María.* (María likes coffee.)

The **a + [person]** part is optional but adds clarity, especially with multiple 3rd-person references.

---

## Negative and Question Forms

| Form | Example |
|:---|:---|
| Negative | *No me gusta el café.* (I don't like coffee.) |
| Question | *¿Te gusta el café?* (Do you like coffee?) |
| Negative question | *¿No te gusta el café?* (Don't you like coffee?) |

---

## Verbs That Follow the Same Pattern

Many Spanish verbs use this "pleasing" construction. They all describe *things affecting the subject*, not the subject acting.

| Spanish | English | Same pattern as gustar |
|:---|:---|:---:|
| **encantar** | to love / really like | ✅ |
| **molestar** | to bother | ✅ |
| **interesar** | to interest | ✅ |
| **importar** | to matter | ✅ |
| **faltar** | to lack / be missing | ✅ |
| **quedar** | to remain / be left | ✅ |
| **doler** | to hurt | ✅ |

### Example sentences

> *Me **encanta** la música.* (I love music.)
> *Le **molesta** el ruido.* (The noise bothers him.)
> *¿Te **interesan** los idiomas?* (Are you interested in languages?)
> *Me **importa** mucho.* (It matters a lot to me.)
> *Te **falta** dinero.* (You're lacking money.)
> *Me **quedan** tres libros.* (I have three books left.)
> *Me **duele** la cabeza.* (My head hurts.)

---

## Common Mistakes for Korean Speakers

1. **Don't translate literally as "I like X."** Spanish reverses the structure — *Me gusta X* is "X pleases me."
2. **gusta vs gustan follows the subject, not the liker.** *Me gustan los libros* (plural subject → gustan), not "gusta."
3. **Infinitive subjects take gusta.** *Me gusta leer* (singular infinitive → gusta).
4. **Don't confuse "gustar" with reflexive.** They look similar but mean different things:
   - *Me gusta el café* (I like coffee) — gustar construction
   - *Me levanto* (I get up) — reflexive verb
5. **Don't overuse "le gusta."** Spanish often repeats the subject explicitly when clarity matters.

## Example Sentences

### Expressing preferences

> *Me gusta la paella.* (I like paella.)
> *¿Te gusta el café o el té?* (Do you like coffee or tea?)
> *A mi madre le gustan las flores.* (My mother likes flowers.)

### Talking about what you enjoy

> *Me gusta leer por la noche.* (I like to read at night.)
> *Nos gusta viajar los fines de semana.* (We like to travel on weekends.)
> *No me gusta el ruido de la ciudad.* (I don't like city noise.)

### With gustar-like verbs

> *Me duelen los pies.* (My feet hurt.)
> *¿Te importa si abro la ventana?* (Do you mind if I open the window?)
> *Le quedan dos días de vacaciones.* (He has two vacation days left.)
