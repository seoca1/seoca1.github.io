# Russian Cyrillic Alphabet (키릴 문자)

> 🇰🇷 **한국어 요약 (Korean Summary)**
> - **핵심:** Russian alphabet = **33 Cyrillic letters** (Korean Hangul 24 + Japanese Hiragana 46, mid-sized).
> - **For Korean speakers:** Hangul = 1 syllable per letter (block); Cyrillic = consonant + vowel joined (like Latin).
> - **Structure:** 21 consonants + 10 vowels + 2 signs (ъ, ь) = 33 letters.
> - **Korean sound mapping:** а=아, б=브, в=브, г=그, д=드, е=예, ж=즈(voiced), з=즈(voiceless), и=이...

## What Is the Cyrillic Alphabet?

Russian is written in the **Cyrillic alphabet (Кириллица)**, derived from Greek script around the 9th century for Slavic languages. Like Korean Hanja (한자), each letter typically represents one syllable.

**For Korean speakers — comparison:**

| Writing System | Korean | Russian | English |
|:---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Letter count | 24 (Hangul) | 33 (Cyrillic) | 26 (Latin) |
| Structure | Syllable blocks | Consonant + vowel | Consonant + vowel |
| Similarity to Latin | Different | Different | (Reference) |

> **Conclusion:** Cyrillic looks unfamiliar at first, but **pronunciation can be learned through Latin-alphabet mapping** (transliteration).

### Special Signs — 2

| Cyrillic | Function | Example | Description |
|:---:|:---|:---|:---|
| Ъ ъ | hard sign | подъезд | marks a hard boundary after consonants |
| Ь ь | soft sign | день | marks palatalization (softening) |

> **ъ, ь are silent** — they signal how the preceding consonant is pronounced (hard vs. soft).

## Sounds Korean Speakers Should Watch Out For

Russian has sounds that don't exist in Korean:

| Cyrillic | Korean Learner Pitfall | Correct Pronunciation |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **Ж ж** | Sounds like Korean "즈" but voiced; round your lips. | /ʒ/ (voiced postalveolar fricative) |
| **Ш ш** | Sounds like Korean "시" but round your lips. | /ʃ/ (voiceless postalveolar fricative) |
| **Щ щ** | Not "shchi" as transliterated — a longer fricative. | /ɕː/ |
| **Х х** | Different from Korean ㅎ — a velar fricative. | /x/ |
| **Р р** | Different from Korean ㄹ — tongue-tip trills against alveolar ridge. | /r/ (trilled) |
| **В в** | Different from Korean ㅂ — labiodental fricative (upper teeth + lower lip). | /v/ |
| **Й й** | Not the Korean vowel ㅣ — a semivowel "y". | /j/ |

## Voiced / Voiceless Pairs

Russian has **voiced/voiceless consonant pairs** (like Korean ㅂ/ㅍ, ㄷ/ㅌ):

| Voiced | Voiceless | Articulation |
|:---:|:---:|:---|
| Б б (b) | П п (p) | Bilabial |
| В в (v) | Ф ф (f) | Labiodental |
| Г г (g) | К к (k) | Velar |
| Д д (d) | Т т (t) | Alveolar |
| Ж ж (zh) | Ш ш (sh) | Postalveolar fricative |
| З з (z) | С с (s) | Alveolar fricative |

> **Korean learner tip:** Distinguish voiced from voiceless like Korean ㅂ/ㅍ. **Word-final devoicing** — voiced consonants become voiceless at the end of a word (e.g. "дуг" → "дук" pronounced). This is **different from Korean** liaison rules.

## Top 5 Mistakes by Korean Speakers

1. **Misreading В в as /b/** — Korean has no /v/ sound. For "вода" (voda, water), lightly touch upper teeth to lower lip and pronounce the fricative.
2. **Trilling Р р incorrectly** — Korean ㄹ is different. Place tongue tip against alveolar ridge and vibrate it.
3. **Confusing Х х with Korean ㅎ** — Russian х is a velar fricative produced at the back of the mouth.
4. **Mixing upper/lower case** — Cyrillic has both. Use uppercase at sentence starts, proper nouns, and names.
5. **Trying to pronounce Ъ ъ and Ь ь** — They are silent. They only mark consonant hardness/palatalization.
