## Lesson Overview

This lesson teaches how to name and describe family members in Spanish. Spanish family vocabulary is rich and gender-specific — many kinship terms change form depending on the gender of the person being referred to (e.g., **tío** vs **tía**, **hermano** vs **hermana**). You'll also learn possessive adjectives (**mi, tu, su**) to describe **whose** family member you're talking about.

**Goal:** Introduce your family in Spanish, describe relationships, ages, and occupations of relatives.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Name 30+ family members in Spanish (immediate, extended, in-laws)
2. Apply gender to family nouns correctly (el tío / la tía)
3. Use possessive adjectives (mi, tu, su, nuestro, vuestro, su)
4. Pluralize family nouns (el hijo → los hijos)
5. Describe a family tree in Spanish
6. Talk about relatives' ages, jobs, and where they live

## Core Grammar 1: Gender in Family Nouns

Most Spanish family nouns come in **masculine/feminine pairs**. The masculine typically ends in **-o** and the feminine in **-a**.

| Masculine | Feminine | English |
|:---|:---|:---|
| el **padre** | la **madre** | father / mother |
| el **hijo** | la **hija** | son / daughter |
| el **hermano** | la **hermana** | brother / sister |
| el **abuelo** | la **abuela** | grandfather / grandmother |
| el **tío** | la **tía** | uncle / aunt |
| el **primo** | la **prima** | cousin (m / f) |
| el **sobrino** | la **sobrina** | nephew / niece |
| el **nieto** | la **nieta** | grandson / granddaughter |
| el **suegro** | la **suegra** | father-in-law / mother-in-law |
| el **yerno** | la **nuera** | son-in-law / daughter-in-law |
| el **cuñado** | la **cuñada** | brother-in-law / sister-in-law |
| el **novio** | la **novia** | boyfriend / girlfriend / fiancé(e) |
| el **esposo** | la **esposa** | husband / wife |

### Plural forms

| Singular | Plural |
|:---|:---|
| el hijo | los **hijos** |
| la hija | las **hijas** |
| el hermano | los **hermanos** |
| el tío | los **tíos** |

> **Korean learner note:** In Korean, family terms change based on **speaker's perspective** (내 형 vs 네 형) and **age relative to speaker** (형 vs 오빠). Spanish instead uses **gender of the relative** (hermano/a) consistently, regardless of speaker. This is a fundamental shift in thinking.

## Core Grammar 2: Possessive Adjectives

Possessives ("my," "your," "his/her") agree with the **possessed noun**, not the possessor.

| Possessor | Singular | Plural | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **my** | **mi** | **mis** | *mi hermano* / *mis hermanos* |
| **your** (tú) | **tu** | **tus** | *tu tía* / *tus tíos* |
| **his/her/your** (Ud.) | **su** | **sus** | *su padre* / *sus padres* |
| **our** | **nuestro/a** | **nuestros/as** | *nuestra casa* / *nuestros primos* |
| **your** (vosotros) | **vuestro/a** | **vuestros/as** | *vuestra hermana* |
| **their/your** (Uds.) | **su** | **sus** | *su familia* / *sus hijos* |

### Examples

> ***Mi** madre es médica.* (My mother is a doctor.)
> ***Mis** hermanos son estudiantes.* (My brothers are students.)
> ***Tu** padre es muy simpático.* (Your father is very nice.)
> ***Nuestra** familia es grande.* (Our family is big.)
> ***Sus** abuelos viven en México.* (Their grandparents live in Mexico.)

> **Korean learner note:** Korean uses different forms for "my older/younger brother" but doesn't change the possessive form itself. Spanish just has mi/tu/su/nuestro/vuestro + gender/number agreement on the noun.

## Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A — Introducing the immediate family

> **A:** ¿Tienes hermanos?
> (Do you have siblings?)
>
> **B:** Sí, tengo dos hermanos y una hermana.
> (Yes, I have two brothers and one sister.)
>
> **A:** ¿Cómo se llaman?
> (What are their names?)
>
> **B:** Mi hermano mayor se llama Pedro, y mi hermana menor se llama Ana.
> (My older brother is named Pedro, and my younger sister is named Ana.)

### Scenario B — Talking about parents

> **A:** ¿A qué se dedica tu padre?
> (What does your father do?)
>
> **B:** Mi padre es ingeniero. Y mi madre es profesora.
> (My father is an engineer. And my mother is a teacher.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y dónde viven tus padres?
> (And where do your parents live?)
>
> **B:** Viven en Seúl, pero mis abuelos viven en Busán.
> (They live in Seoul, but my grandparents live in Busan.)

### Scenario C — Family tree

> **A:** ¿Quién es tu tía Rosa?
> (Who is your Aunt Rosa?)
>
> **B:** Es la hermana de mi madre. Tiene dos hijos, mis primos Luis y Marta.
> (She's my mother's sister. She has two children, my cousins Luis and Marta.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y tu tío Juan?
> (And your Uncle Juan?)
>
> **B:** Es el hermano de mi padre. Está casado con mi tía Carmen.
> (He's my father's brother. He's married to my Aunt Carmen.)

### Scenario D — Talking about in-laws

> **A:** ¿Cómo se llama tu suegra?
> (What's your mother-in-law's name?)
>
> **B:** Se llama María. Es muy simpática.
> (Her name is María. She's very nice.)
>
> **A:** ¿Tienes cuñados?
> (Do you have brothers-in-law/sisters-in-law?)
>
> **B:** Sí, tengo un cuñado y dos cuñadas.
> (Yes, I have one brother-in-law and two sisters-in-law.)

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|

## Pronunciation Guide

### Tricky sounds for Korean speakers

| Spanish sound | Example | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **ñ** [ɲ] | niño, año, señor | like "ny" in "canyon"; Korean ㅄ과 유사 |
| **j** [x] or [h] | hijo, ajo | strong breathy "h"; Korean ㅎ보다 강함 |
| **r** (initial) | primo, hermano | single tap |
| **rr** | suegra | rolled trill |
| **v / b** | abuelo, novio | pronounced the same in modern Spanish |
| **d** (between vowels) | suegra, padre | softer [ð] sound |

### In connected speech

> *Mi madre* → [mi ˈmaðɾe]
> *Tu hermana* → [tu eɾˈmana]
> *Su abuelo* → [su aˈβuelo]
> *Nuestros primos* → [ˈnwestɾos ˈpɾimos]

> **Korean learner note:** Practice **hijo** [ˈixo] — the **j** is the trickiest. Say "hi" while pushing more air out. Then add **-o**: "hi-ho."

## Notes for Korean Speakers

### 1. No honorific layers in nouns themselves

In Korean, "형" vs "오빠" vs "남동생" depends on the speaker's perspective and age relative to the speaker. Spanish simplifies this: **hermano** for all brothers. Gender of the brother doesn't matter — only the gender of the noun (masculine).

### 2. Korean kinship uses 반말 (intimate) for everyone

In Korean, family terms themselves imply intimacy (오빠 is informal). Spanish family nouns are **neutral** — formality comes from **how** you describe them, not the noun itself.

### 3. Possessive + kinship pattern

Korean says "우리 엄마" (we mom) for "my mom" — **우리** is grammatically "our" but means "my" with family. Spanish uses **mi** straightforwardly. Don't confuse yourself by translating literally.

### 4. The "Suegro" family cluster

Spanish is unique in having separate nouns for in-laws:

- suegro / suegra (father/mother-in-law)
- yerno / nuera (son/daughter-in-law)
- cuñado / cuñada (brother/sister-in-law)

Korean uses the same base noun + 관계 (e.g., "아내의 어머니" = suegra). Spanish requires memorizing these specific words.

### 5. "Tío" doubles as slang for "guy/dude"

In modern Spanish (especially Spain), **tío** can mean "dude" or "guy" casually, similar to English "bro." Context matters.
