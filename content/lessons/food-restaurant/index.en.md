## Lesson Overview

This lesson teaches how to talk about food and order in a Spanish-speaking restaurant. It covers **food vocabulary by category**, **key restaurant verbs** (pedir, querer, probar), **the gustar construction for preferences**, and **regional dining customs**. The lesson complements [Restaurant Ordering](/lessons/restaurant-ordering/) with broader vocabulary; that lesson focuses on the polite ordering process in depth.

**Goal:** Confidently discuss food preferences, order at restaurants, understand menu items, and navigate Spanish-speaking dining culture.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Name 60+ food items by category (fruits, vegetables, meats, drinks, desserts)
2. Use **gustar** to express food preferences (me gusta, me gustan)
3. Apply **querer** / **quisiera** to order food politely
4. Use **pedir** (to order), **probar** (to try), **pagar** (to pay)
5. Describe dishes using adjectives (delicioso, picante, dulce, salado)
6. Distinguish countable vs uncountable nouns (un café vs café)
7. Navigate regional meal-time differences (Spain vs Latin America)

## Core Grammar 1: Articles with Food

Unlike English, Spanish requires **articles** before most food nouns. The article must match the noun's gender.

| Pattern | Singular | Plural | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Masculine **-o** | **el** arroz | **los** arroces | (z → c before -es) |
| Feminine **-a** | **la** manzana | **las** manzanas | |
| Masculine consonant | **el** pan | **los** panes | |
| Feminine consonant | **la** carne | **las** carnes | |

### Examples

> ***El** arroz está delicioso.* (The rice is delicious.)
> ***La** carne es muy buena.* (The meat is very good.)
> ***Los** champiñones son frescos.* (The mushrooms are fresh.)
> ***Las** fresas son dulces.* (The strawberries are sweet.)

---

## Core Grammar 2: Gustar for Food Preferences

The **gustar** construction reverses the English order: "I like X" = "X is pleasing to me."

```
indirect pronoun + gusta/gustan + food
```

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|
| ***Me gusta** la paella.* | I like paella. |
| ***Me gustan** las fresas.* | I like strawberries. |
| ***Te gusta** el café.* | You like coffee. |
| ***Le gusta** el chocolate.* | He/she likes chocolate. |
| ***Nos gusta** la comida mexicana.* | We like Mexican food. |
| ***No me gusta** la cebolla.* | I don't like onions. |

> **Singular vs plural verb form:** **gusta** (singular food) / **gustan** (plural food).

---

## Core Grammar 3: Quisiera vs Quiero

Both mean "I want" but **quisiera** is more polite (the conditional form).

| Form | Tone | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| ***Quiero** un café.* | direct, casual | I want a coffee. |
| ***Quisiera** un café, por favor.* | polite ✅ | I would like a coffee, please. |
| ***Me gustaría** probar el vino.* | very polite | I would like to try the wine. |

## Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A — Discussing food preferences

> **A:** ¿Qué te gusta comer?
> (What do you like to eat?)
>
> **B:** Me gusta mucho la comida mexicana. Me gustan los tacos y las enchiladas.
> (I really like Mexican food. I like tacos and enchiladas.)
>
> **A:** ¿No te gusta la comida picante?
> (Don't you like spicy food?)
>
> **B:** Sí, me gusta el picante. ¿Y a ti?
> (Yes, I like spicy food. And you?)
>
> **A:** A mí me gusta más la comida italiana. Me encantan las pastas.
> (I like Italian food more. I love pastas.)

### Scenario B — Ordering at a restaurant

> **Waiter:** Buenas tardes. ¿Qué van a tomar?
> (Good afternoon. What will you have?)
>
> **Customer:** Para mí, la paella, por favor.
> (For me, the paella, please.)
>
> **Customer:** Y para beber, una copa de vino tinto.
> (And to drink, a glass of red wine.)
>
> **Waiter:** ¿Quieren ver el menú de postres?
> (Do you want to see the dessert menu?)
>
> **Customer:** Sí, por favor. Quisiera probar el flan.
> (Yes, please. I'd like to try the flan.)

### Scenario C — At the market / grocery store

> **Customer:** ¿Cuánto cuestan las fresas?
> (How much are the strawberries?)
>
> **Vendor:** Las fresas cuestan tres euros el kilo.
> (The strawberries are 3 euros per kilo.)
>
> **Customer:** Quiero medio kilo de fresas y un kilo de manzanas.
> (I want half a kilo of strawberries and a kilo of apples.)
>
> **Vendor:** Son ocho euros con cincuenta.
> (That's 8 euros 50.)
>
> **Customer:** Aquí tiene. ¿Acepta tarjeta?
> (Here you go. Do you accept card?)

### Scenario D — Asking about ingredients

> **Customer:** Perdón, ¿este plato tiene gluten?
> (Excuse me, does this dish have gluten?)
>
> **Waiter:** No, no tiene gluten. Pero tiene leche y nueces.
> (No, it doesn't have gluten. But it has milk and nuts.)
>
> **Customer:** Perfecto. Soy alérgico a los mariscos.
> (Perfect. I'm allergic to seafood.)
>
> **Waiter:** No se preocupe. Este plato es vegetariano, sin mariscos.
> (Don't worry. This dish is vegetarian, no seafood.)

### Scenario E — Asking for recommendations

> **Customer:** ¿Qué recomienda de la casa?
> (What do you recommend from the house?)
>
> **Waiter:** El especial de hoy es el cordero asado con verduras.
> (Today's special is roast lamb with vegetables.)
>
> **Customer:** Suena bien. Quisiera probarlo.
> (Sounds good. I'd like to try it.)
>
> **Waiter:** Excelente elección. Se lo preparo enseguida.
> (Excellent choice. I'll prepare it right away.)

## Notes for Korean Speakers

### 1. Korean has no article system; Spanish requires articles

Korean just says "밥" (rice). Spanish requires **el arroz** (the rice) or **arroz** (rice as uncountable mass). Korean learners often drop articles — practice adding them until automatic.

### 2. Countable vs uncountable distinction

Korean doesn't strongly mark this. Spanish does:

- **Uncountable:** el arroz (rice), el pan (bread), la leche (milk) — no plural
- **Countable:** la manzana (apple), el huevo (egg) — has plural

### 3. "Agua" is feminine but takes masculine article

Korean has no gender system, so this is uniquely Spanish. **El agua** (masc. article + fem. noun) because of stress on first syllable. Plural returns to feminine: **las aguas**.

### 4. Gustar reverses English structure

Korean follows English order: "나는 커피를 좋아해" (I-coffee-like). Spanish reverses: "Me gusta el café" (to-me pleases the coffee). Major structural shift — practice until natural.

### 5. Meal times are very different

Korean lunch: 12:00-13:00. Spanish lunch: 14:00-16:00. Spanish dinner: 21:00-23:00. Eating habits are cultural — adapt your schedule when in Spain or Latin America.

### 6. "Ñ" is critical for food vocabulary

Many food words have ñ: champiñón, albañil-style words. The ñ sound [ɲ] doesn't exist in Korean but is similar to ㅄ from "이" + 받침. Practice these food words specifically.

### 7. Restaurants may add "10% servicio" (service charge)

Spain often includes service charge. Argentina doesn't. Mexico tips 10-15%. Korea doesn't tip. Know the customs to avoid confusion.
