## Lesson Overview

This lesson teaches how to count from 1 to 100 in Spanish. Spanish numbers follow a **base-10 system** similar to English, but with important pronunciation, accent, and gender patterns that English speakers (and Korean speakers) often overlook.

**Goal:** Count fluently to 100, pronounce all numbers correctly, and use them in real-life contexts (prices, phone numbers, ages, dates).

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Count from 1 to 100 in Spanish
2. Pronounce every number correctly (z/c distinction, accent marks)
3. Identify the **decena + y + unidad** pattern for 31–99
4. Distinguish **cien** (exactly 100) vs **ciento** (101+)
5. Apply numbers in real contexts: prices, ages, phone numbers, dates, addresses

---

## Core Structure: How Spanish Numbers Work

Spanish numbers follow three building blocks:

```
1-15      → unique words (must memorize)
16-29     → combination patterns
30-99     → decena (tens) + y + unidad (ones)
100+      → ciento... (101-199) / doscientos... (200+)
```

The biggest hurdle for English/Korean speakers is the **16-29 range**, which doesn't follow a single rule.

## Numbers 16-29 (Three Sub-Patterns)

| Range | Pattern | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 16-19 | **dieci- + unidad** (one word) | dieciséis, diecisiete, dieciocho, diecinueve |
| 20-29 | **veinti- + unidad** (one word, accent removed when combined) | veintiuno, veintidós, veintitrés |

### Numbers 16-19 — dieci- prefix

| Number | Spanish | Pronunciation |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| 16 | **dieciséis** | [dje.θiˈsejs] / [dje.siˈsejs] |
| 17 | **diecisiete** | [dje.θiˈsje.te] |
| 18 | **dieciocho** | [djeˈθjo.tʃo] |
| 19 | **diecinueve** | [dje.θiˈnwe.βe] |

> ⚠️ **Stress shift:** dieciséis, diecisiete, diecinueve carry an accent on the **last syllable** because the stress naturally falls there.

### Numbers 20-29 — veinti- prefix

| Number | Spanish | Pronunciation |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| 20 | **veinte** | [ˈbein.te] |
| 21 | **veintiuno** | [beinˈtju.no] |
| 22 | **veintidós** | [beinˈti.ðos] |
| 23 | **veintitrés** | [beinˈti.tɾes] |
| 24 | **veinticuatro** | [bein.tiˈkwa.tɾo] |
| 25 | **veinticinco** | [beinˈtiŋ.ko] |
| 26 | **veintiséis** | [beinˈtisejs] |
| 27 | **veintisiete** | [beinˈtiˈsje.te] |
| 28 | **veintiocho** | [beinˈtjo.tʃo] |
| 29 | **veintinueve** | [beinˈtiˈnwe.βe] |

> **Korean learner note:** When veinte (20) combines with a unit, the final **-e** drops and the unit word attaches directly: veinti-uno, veinti-dós. From 21-29, **only one word** — no space, no "y".

## Numbers 31-99 (Pattern: decena + y + unidad)

For any number from 31 to 99, use this formula:

```
decena + y + unidad
```

| Number | Formula | Spanish |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| 31 | treinta y uno | treinta y uno |
| 32 | treinta y dos | treinta y dos |
| 42 | cuarenta y dos | cuarenta y dos |
| 55 | cincuenta y cinco | cincuenta y cinco |
| 67 | sesenta y siete | sesenta y siete |
| 78 | setenta y ocho | setenta y ocho |
| 84 | ochenta y cuatro | ochenta y cuatro |
| 99 | noventa y nueve | noventa y nueve |

> ⚠️ **Gender rule:** When **uno** appears as the last word (e.g., 31, 41, 51), it must agree with the gender of the noun: **treinta y un libros** (masc.) / **treinta y una mesas** (fem.).

## Number 100: Cien vs Ciento

| Number | Spanish | Usage |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| Exactly 100 | **cien** | before nouns, or alone |
| 101-199 | **ciento + número** | ciento uno, ciento veinte, ciento noventa y nueve |
| 200 | **doscientos / doscientas** | agree with gender of following noun |
| 300 | **trescientos / trescientas** | agree with gender |
| 500 | **quinientos / quinientas** | special spelling change |
| 700 | **setecientos / setecientas** | special spelling change |
| 900 | **novecientos / novecientas** | special spelling change |

### Examples

> *Hay **cien** estudiantes en la clase.* (There are 100 students in the class.)
> *Tengo **ciento uno** años.* (I am 101 years old.) — colloquial
> ***Doscientas** personas vinieron.* (200 people came — feminine plural)

## Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A — Talking about age

> **A:** ¿Cuántos años tienes?
> (How old are you?)
>
> **B:** Tengo veinticinco años.
> (I'm 25 years old.)
>
> **A:** Y tu hermana, ¿cuántos años tiene?
> (And your sister, how old is she?)
>
> **B:** Mi hermana tiene treinta y dos años.
> (My sister is 32.)

### Scenario B — Buying something

> **A:** ¿Cuánto cuesta?
> (How much does it cost?)
>
> **B:** Cuesta quince euros.
> (It costs 15 euros.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y este otro?
> (And this other one?)
>
> **B:** Éste cuesta veintitrés euros con cincuenta céntimos.
> (This one costs 23 euros and 50 cents.)

### Scenario C — Phone number

> **A:** ¿Cuál es tu número de teléfono?
> (What's your phone number?)
>
> **B:** Es el seis, dos, ocho, cinco, cero, cuatro, tres, nueve.
> (It's 6-2-8-5-0-4-3-9.)

### Scenario D — Dates

> **A:** ¿Cuándo es tu cumpleaños?
> (When is your birthday?)
>
> **B:** Es el quince de marzo.
> (It's March 15th.)
>
> **A:** ¡Felicidades! Cumples veintiocho, ¿verdad?
> (Congratulations! You're turning 28, right?)

## Pronunciation Guide

### Spain vs Latin America (z, c distinction)

| Letter | Spain | Latin America |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **z** (cinco, diez) | [θ] (like "th" in "think") | [s] |
| **c** before e/i (cinco, once) | [θ] | [s] |
| **c** before a/o/u (cuatro, ochenta) | [k] | [k] |

> **Korean learner tip:** Korean has neither [θ] nor [s] as in Spanish. Practice by placing your tongue between your teeth for [θ] (Spain) or behind your teeth for [s] (LatAm).

### Stress and accents

| Word | Stress | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **dieciséis** | last syllable | acute accent (´) marks stress |
| **veintidós** | last syllable | acute accent |
| **cuarenta** | second-to-last | natural stress, no accent |
| **cincuenta** | second-to-last | natural stress, no accent |

### Number rhythm drill

> *Uno, dos, tres, cuatro, cinco, seis, siete, ocho, nueve, diez.*
> *Once, doce, trece, catorce, quince.*
> *Dieciséis, diecisiete, dieciocho, diecinueve.*
> *Veinte, veintiuno, veintidós… veintinueve.*
> *Treinta, cuarenta, cincuenta, sesenta, setenta, ochenta, noventa.*
> *Cien.*

## Notes for Korean Speakers

### 1. Sino-Korean number interference

Korean has two number systems: **Sino-Korean** (일, 이, 삼…) used for dates and money, and **Native Korean** (하나, 둘, 셋…) used for ages and counts. Spanish has only **one** number system — and unlike Korean, Spanish nouns have **gender**, which affects adjacent numbers.

| Korean | Spanish | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 일 (Sino) | uno | single number system |
| 한 (Native) | un / una | agree with noun gender |

### 2. Korean has no equivalent to "dieci-" and "veinti-"

In Korean, 16 is simply "십육" (10 + 6) — always two syllables. In Spanish, 16-29 are **single words** (dieciséis, veintiuno). This is unique to Spanish among Romance languages (compare French: seize, Italian: sedici — single words, but Portuguese: dezesseis — compound).

### 3. Cien vs ciento = unique

No Korean equivalent. Memorize: **cien** = exactly 100 (alone or before noun), **ciento** = 101-199.

### 4. Number agreement with noun

Korean numerals don't change form. Spanish numerals **agree** with nouns in **gender and number** (for uno and compounds ending in -uno):

> *treinta y un libros* (masc. pl.)
> *treinta y una mesas* (fem. pl.)
> *ciento una páginas* (fem. sg.)

This is a fundamental difference. Practice until automatic.

### 5. Pronunciation: the [θ] vs [s] split

Korean doesn't have either sound natively. Use this trick:
- For **Spain pronunciation**: bite your tongue lightly and blow → [θ]
- For **Latin American pronunciation**: tongue behind teeth → [s]
