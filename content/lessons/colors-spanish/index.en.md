# Colors (Los Colores)



## Lesson Overview

This lesson teaches how to name colors in Spanish and use them as adjectives to describe objects. Spanish color adjectives must **agree in gender and number** with the noun they modify — making them slightly more complex than English color words but extremely useful for everyday conversation.

**Goal:** Name 23+ colors, apply correct gender/number agreement, and describe objects, clothing, and people using color adjectives.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Name 23+ colors in Spanish (basic, extended, descriptive)
2. Apply gender agreement to color adjectives (rojo → roja, rojo → rojos, roja → rojas)
3. Distinguish singular vs plural color forms
4. Use **claro** (light) / **oscuro** (dark) modifiers
5. Describe everyday objects, clothing, and people by color
6. Use colors in conversation about preferences and appearance

---

## Core Grammar 1: Color Adjective Agreement

Unlike English, Spanish color adjectives **change form** based on the noun's **gender** and **number**.

### Agreement patterns

| Pattern | Singular masc. | Singular fem. | Plural masc. | Plural fem. |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **-o → -a → -os → -as** | rojo | roja | rojos | rojas |
| **-e → -es** (no gender change) | verde | verde | verdes | verdes |
| **-ista → -istas** (no gender change) | celeste | celeste | celestes | celestes |
| **consonant + -es** | azul | azul | azules | azules |

> **Key rule:** Colors ending in **-o** change to **-a** for feminine nouns. Colors ending in **-e**, a **consonant**, or **-ista** stay the same.

### Examples in context

> *El coche es **rojo**.* (The car is red — masculine singular)
> *La casa es **roja**.* (The house is red — feminine singular)
> *Los coches son **rojos**.* (The cars are red — masculine plural)
> *Las casas son **rojas**.* (The houses are red — feminine plural)

> *El cielo es **azul**.* (The sky is blue — same form for masc. and fem.)
> *Los ojos son **azules**.* (The eyes are blue — plural adds -es)

---

## Core Grammar 2: Where Colors Go in a Sentence

In Spanish, descriptive adjectives (including colors) typically go **after** the noun.

```
noun + color adjective
```

| English (adj before noun) | Spanish (adj after noun) |
|:---|:---|
| red car | el coche **rojo** |
| blue house | la casa **azul** |
| white shirts | las camisas **blancas** |
| green book | el libro **verde** |

> **Korean learner note:** Korean uses postpositional particles (e.g., 빨간 차, 파란 하늘). Spanish also places the adjective **after** the noun — different from English but similar in spirit to Korean.

| Spanish | English | Masc. sg. | Fem. sg. | Plural form |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **rojo** | red | rojo | roja | rojos / rojas |
| **azul** | blue | azul | azul | azules |
| **amarillo** | yellow | amarillo | amarilla | amarillos / amarillas |
| **verde** | green | verde | verde | verdes |
| **blanco** | white | blanco | blanca | blancos / blancas |
| **negro** | black | negro | negra | negros / negras |
| **gris** | gray | gris | gris | grises |
| **marrón** | brown | marrón | marrón | marrones |
| **rosa** | pink | rosa | rosa | rosas |
| **naranja** | orange | naranja | naranja | naranjas |
| **morado / púrpura** | purple | morado | morada | morados / moradas |

> **Special notes:**
> - **rosa** and **naranja** are invariable in the singular (they end in **-a**, but the masculine form is the same word). Plural adds **-s**: rosas, naranjas.
> - **marrón** adds **-es** in plural: marrones.
> - **gris** adds **-es** in plural: grises.

## Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A — Describing a person

> **A:** ¿Cómo es tu hermano?
> (What does your brother look like?)
>
> **B:** Mi hermano es alto y rubio. Tiene los ojos azules.
> (My brother is tall and blond. He has blue eyes.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y tu hermana?
> (And your sister?)
>
> **B:** Mi hermana es baja y morena. Tiene el pelo castaño.
> (My sister is short and dark-haired. She has brown hair.)

### Scenario B — Shopping for clothes

> **Customer:** Quiero una camisa azul.
> (I want a blue shirt.)
>
> **Vendor:** ¿Azul claro u oscuro?
> (Light blue or dark?)
>
> **Customer:** Azul claro, por favor. ¿Tiene en talla mediana?
> (Light blue, please. Do you have it in medium?)
>
> **Vendor:** Sí, aquí tiene.
> (Yes, here you go.)

### Scenario C — Describing a painting

> **A:** ¿De qué color es el cuadro?
> (What color is the painting?)
>
> **B:** Es un cuadro muy colorido. Tiene rojo, amarillo, azul y verde.
> (It's a very colorful painting. It has red, yellow, blue, and green.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y el fondo?
> (And the background?)
>
> **B:** El fondo es negro.
> (The background is black.)

### Scenario D — Asking about someone's favorite color

> **A:** ¿Cuál es tu color favorito?
> (What's your favorite color?)
>
> **B:** Mi color favorito es el azul. ¿Y el tuyo?
> (My favorite color is blue. And yours?)
>
> **A:** A mí me gusta el verde.
> (I like green.)

> **Note:** When expressing preferences about colors, use **el + color**: *Me gusta **el** rojo* (I like red). Color words become masculine nouns in this construction.

## Pronunciation Guide

### Color words that change with context

| Word | Pronunciation | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **rojo** | [ˈro.xo] | j = [x] (strong h) |
| **roja** | [ˈro.xa] | same root, -a |
| **azul** | [aˈsul] | stress on last syllable |
| **verde** | [ˈbeɾ.ðe] | d between vowels = [ð] (soft th) |
| **blanco** | [ˈblan.ko] | |
| **amarillo** | [a.maˈɾi.ʎo] | ll = [ʎ] (like "ly") |
| **gris** | [ɡɾis] | r before s = single tap |
| **marrón** | [maˈron] | ó = stressed o |
| **naranja** | [naˈɾan.xa] | j = [x] |

### Trick: the "colorless" vowels

When a color adjective ends in **-e** or a **consonant**, the spoken form is the same regardless of gender. Listen for the **noun**'s article (el/la) to know which is which.

> *El coche **verde*** [el ˈkotʃe ˈbeɾðe] — masculine noun
> *La casa **verde*** [la ˈkasa ˈbeɾðe] — feminine noun
> The word **verde** sounds identical.

## Notes for Korean Speakers

### 1. Korean -색 (color noun suffix) doesn't exist in Spanish

Korean often creates color nouns by adding -색: 빨강**색**, 파랑**색**, 노랑**색**. Spanish has only **adjectives** — no "-색" equivalent. To use a color as a noun, add the article:

> 빨간색 → **el rojo** (the red)
> 파란색 → **el azul** (the blue)
> 내 색깔 → **mi color** (my color — general)

### 2. Korean has no adjective agreement

Korean 색깔 adjectives don't change form (빨간 stays 빨간). Spanish **requires** gender/number agreement. This is a major structural shift. Practice until automatic.

### 3. Korean has separate Sino-Korean vs Native color words

Korean has 빨강 (Sino) and 빨갛다 (native adjective). Spanish has just one word per color. No dual system.

### 4. "Verde" has a special idiomatic meaning

**Estar verde** = to be inexperienced (in Spain). **Poner verde a alguien** = to insult someone. Context matters.

### 5. Hair color vocabulary in Spanish is detailed

Korean often uses 단발 (short), 긴 (long) for hair. Spanish uses **rubio/a** (blond), **pelirrojo/a** (redhead), **castaño/a** (brown), **moreno/a** (brunette) — much more granular.
