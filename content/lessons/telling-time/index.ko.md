> 🇰🇷 **한국어 요약 (Korean Summary)**
> - **핵심:** 시간 표현 — Es la una / Son las dos… + 15분(y cuarto) / 30분(y media) / 45분(menos cuarto)

## Lesson Overview

This lesson teaches how to tell time in Spanish, including hours, half-hours, quarter-hours, AM/PM distinctions, and time-related frequency expressions. Spanish time-telling differs from English in three key ways: (1) **singular vs plural** for 1:00 vs other hours, (2) **a la** (at) instead of "at," and (3) **cuarto/media** constructions instead of "quarter past/half past."

**Goal:** Tell time fluently, understand time expressions, schedule events, and discuss daily schedules.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Tell time on the hour, half-hour, and quarter-hour
2. Use **Es la una** vs **Son las dos...** correctly
3. Apply **y cuarto** (15 past) / **y media** (half past) / **menos cuarto** (quarter to)
4. Distinguish **de la mañana** (AM) / **de la tarde** (PM) / **de la noche** (night)
5. Use **¿A qué hora?** (at what time?) question form
6. Talk about schedules and frequency (siempre, a veces, nunca)
7. Write formal time (24-hour clock) and informal time (12-hour clock)

---

## Core Grammar 1: Es la una vs Son las...

Spanish uses two different verbs for telling time based on whether the hour is **1** or **other numbers**.

| Hour | Spanish | Why |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| 1:00 | **Es la** una | singular — **la** refers to **la hora** (one hour) |
| 2:00, 3:00... | **Son las** dos, tres... | plural — referring to **las horas** (multiple hours) |

> **The rule:** **1:00 = Es la una**. **All other hours = Son las + número**.

### The basic pattern

```
Son las + [number] + [minutes if any]
```

| Time | Spanish | Literal |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 1:00 | **Es la una.** | It is the one. |
| 2:00 | **Son las dos.** | They are the two. |
| 3:00 | **Son las tres.** | They are the three. |
| 12:00 | **Son las doce.** | They are the twelve. |
| 13:00 (1 PM) | **Es la una.** | (back to singular) |

## Core Grammar 2: Minutes Past the Hour

### Quarter past / half past / quarter to

| English | Spanish | Structure |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 1:15 | **Es la una y cuarto.** | 1 + **y cuarto** (and quarter) |
| 1:30 | **Es la una y media.** | 1 + **y media** (and half) |
| 1:45 | **Son las dos menos cuarto.** | 2 **menos cuarto** (minus quarter) |
| 2:15 | **Son las dos y cuarto.** | 2 + **y cuarto** |
| 2:30 | **Son las dos y media.** | 2 + **y media** |
| 2:45 | **Son las tres menos cuarto.** | 3 **menos cuarto** |

> **Korean learner note:** Korean says "1시 15분" (1 hour 15 minutes). Spanish uses **y cuarto** for "quarter past" and **menos cuarto** for "quarter to" — borrowed from clock positions. Memorize these as fixed phrases.

### Other minutes

For minutes not on the quarter-hour, use **y + minutes**:

| Time | Spanish |
|:---|:---|
| 1:05 | **Es la una y cinco.** |
| 1:10 | **Es la una y diez.** |
| 1:20 | **Es la una y veinte.** |
| 1:25 | **Es la una y veinticinco.** |
| 1:35 | **Son las dos menos veinticinco.** |
| 1:40 | **Son las dos menos veinte.** |
| 1:50 | **Son las dos menos diez.** |
| 1:55 | **Son las dos menos cinco.** |

> **Pattern:** When minutes are 31-59, Spanish counts **down** to the next hour using **menos** (less/minus).

## Core Grammar 3: AM/PM Distinctions

Spanish distinguishes parts of the day with specific phrases:

| Time of day | Spanish | Usage |
|:---|:---|:---|
| morning (AM, after midnight) | **de la mañana** | 1 AM - 12 PM |
| afternoon (PM) | **de la tarde** | 1 PM - 8 PM |
| evening/night (PM) | **de la noche** | 8 PM - 12 AM |
| dawn (early morning) | **de la madrugada** | 12 AM - 6 AM |

### Examples

> *Son las siete **de la mañana**.* (7 AM — It is 7 in the morning.)
> *Son las tres **de la tarde**.* (3 PM — It is 3 in the afternoon.)
> *Son las diez **de la noche**.* (10 PM — It is 10 at night.)
> *Son las dos **de la madrugada**.* (2 AM — It is 2 in the early morning.)

### 24-hour format (formal)

For schedules (planes, trains, official events), use the 24-hour clock without the AM/PM marker:

> *El tren sale a las **14:30**.* (The train leaves at 14:30 / 2:30 PM.)
> *La reunión es a las **09:00**.* (The meeting is at 9:00.)

> **Korean learner note:** Korean uses 오전/오후 (AM/PM) explicitly. Spanish uses **de la mañana/tarde/noche** as soft contextual markers. In formal writing, the 24-hour clock is preferred.

## Asking and Answering About Time

### Key questions

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|
| *¿Qué hora es?* | What time is it? |
| *¿A qué hora?* | At what time? |
| *¿A qué hora sales?* | What time do you leave? |
| *¿A qué hora es la reunión?* | What time is the meeting? |
| *¿Tienes la hora?* | Do you have the time? |
| *¿Sabes qué hora es?* | Do you know what time it is? |

### Key answers

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|
| *Son las tres.* | It's 3 o'clock. |
| *Es la una y cuarto.* | It's 1:15. |
| *Son las cinco y media.* | It's 5:30. |
| *Son las diez menos cinco.* | It's 9:55. |
| *No sé.* | I don't know. |

### Preposition "a"

When saying **at what time** for an event, use **a + las + time**:

> ***A** las ocho* (At 8 o'clock)
> ***A** la una* (At 1 o'clock — singular)
> ***A** las tres de la tarde* (At 3 in the afternoon)

> *La clase es **a las nueve**.* (The class is at 9.)
> *El vuelo es **a las seis de la tarde**.* (The flight is at 6 PM.)
> *Cenamos **a las ocho y media**.* (We have dinner at 8:30.)

## Time Frequency Expressions

| Spanish | English | Frequency |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **siempre** | always | 100% |
| **casi siempre** | almost always | ~90% |
| **muchas veces** | many times / often | ~70% |
| **a veces** | sometimes | ~50% |
| **de vez en cuando** | from time to time | ~30% |
| **rara vez** | rarely | ~10% |
| **casi nunca** | almost never | ~5% |
| **nunca** / **jamás** | never | 0% |

### Examples

> ***Siempre** me levanto a las siete.* (I always get up at 7.)
> ***A veces** estudio por la noche.* (Sometimes I study at night.)
> ***Nunca** como a las tres de la madrugada.* (I never eat at 3 AM.)

### Position in the sentence

Frequency adverbs typically go **right after the verb** (or at the start for emphasis):

> *Me levanto **siempre** a las siete.* (less natural)
> ***Siempre** me levanto a las siete.* (emphasizes "always")
> *Me **siempre** levanto a las siete.* ❌ (wrong word order)

## Conversation Scenarios

### Scenario A — Asking the time

> **A:** Perdón, ¿tiene la hora?
> (Excuse me, do you have the time?)
>
> **B:** Sí, son las tres y cuarto.
> (Yes, it's 3:15.)
>
> **A:** Gracias.
> (Thank you.)
>
> **B:** De nada.
> (You're welcome.)

### Scenario B — Scheduling a meeting

> **A:** ¿A qué hora es la reunión?
> (What time is the meeting?)
>
> **B:** Es a las diez de la mañana.
> (It's at 10 AM.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y hasta qué hora?
> (And until what time?)
>
> **B:** Hasta las doce y media.
> (Until 12:30.)
>
> **A:** Perfecto, nos vemos a las diez.
> (Perfect, see you at 10.)

### Scenario C — Talking about daily schedule

> **A:** ¿A qué hora te levantas?
> (What time do you get up?)
>
> **B:** Me levanto a las seis y media, y llego al trabajo a las ocho menos cuarto.
> (I get up at 6:30, and I arrive at work at 7:45.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y a qué hora sales?
> (And what time do you leave?)
>
> **B:** Salgo a las cinco de la tarde, casi siempre.
> (I leave at 5 PM, almost always.)

### Scenario D — Describing a routine with frequency

> **A:** ¿Con qué frecuencia haces ejercicio?
> (How often do you exercise?)
>
> **B:** Hago ejercicio tres veces por semana, siempre a las seis de la mañana.
> (I exercise three times a week, always at 6 AM.)
>
> **A:** ¿Y nunca haces ejercicio por la noche?
> (And you never exercise at night?)
>
> **B:** Casi nunca. Es más difícil por la noche.
> (Almost never. It's harder at night.)

## Useful Time Phrases

### Days of the week (los días de la semana)

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|
| **lunes** | Monday |
| **martes** | Tuesday |
| **miércoles** | Wednesday |
| **jueves** | Thursday |
| **viernes** | Friday |
| **sábado** | Saturday |
| **domingo** | Sunday |

### Days + frequency

> *Los **lunes** voy al gimnasio.* (On Mondays I go to the gym.)
> *Todos los **días** me levanto temprano.* (Every day I wake up early.)
> *Los **fines de semana** descanso.* (On the weekends I rest.)

### Time intervals

| Spanish | English |
|:---|:---|
| **una hora** | one hour |
| **media hora** | half an hour |
| **un cuarto de hora** | a quarter of an hour |
| **un minuto** | one minute |
| **un segundo** | one second |

### Time durations

> ***¿Cuánto tiempo** dura la clase?* (How long does the class last?)
> ***¿Desde cuándo** estudias español?* (Since when have you studied Spanish?)
> ***¿Hasta cuándo** te quedas?* (Until when are you staying?)

## Pronunciation Guide

### Linking in time expressions

In Spanish, words run together naturally. Time expressions link closely:

> *Son las tres y cuarto* → [ˈson las tɾes i ˈkwaɾto]
> *Es la una y media* → [ˈes la ˈuna i ˈmeðja]
> *Son las dos menos cuarto* → [ˈson las ˈdos ˈmenos ˈkwaɾto]
> *A las ocho de la noche* → [a las ˈo.tʃo ðe la ˈno.tʃe]

### Tricky sounds

| Sound | Example | Note |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **y** as conjunction | *Son las dos **y** media.* | pronounced [i] (like English "ee") |
| **y** as letter | *¿Y tú?* | same [i] sound |
| **cuarto** | *las dos **y cuarto*** | stress on first syllable |
| **media** | *la una **y media*** | stress on first syllable |
| **mediodía** | *Son las doce del **mediodía**.* | stress on second syllable [meðjoˈðia] |

> **Korean learner note:** "y" in time expressions is pronounced like English "ee" [i]. Don't pronounce it as in English "yes" [j] — that error is common.

## Notes for Korean Speakers

### 1. Singular vs plural at 1:00

Korean just says "한 시" (1 o'clock) — singular form not particularly highlighted. Spanish makes **Es la una** (singular feminine article) a unique exception. Every other hour uses **Son las + plural**. Memorize this rule.

### 2. "Cuarto" and "media" instead of "quarter" and "half"

Korean says "1시 15분" and "1시 30분" — explicit minutes. Spanish uses **y cuarto** (and quarter) and **y media** (and half). These are fixed phrases — memorize as chunks.

### 3. "Menos" for 31-59 minutes

Korean counts forward: "1시 35분" = 1 hour 35 minutes. Spanish counts **backward** after 30: "1시 35분" = **Son las dos menos veinticinco** (2 minus 25). This is fundamentally different. Practice until natural.

### 4. No explicit "AM/PM" markers

Korean uses 오전/오후. Spanish uses **de la mañana/tarde/noche** as soft contextual cues. In conversation, context usually makes AM/PM clear. For formal schedules, use 24-hour clock.

### 5. Frequency word order

Korean places 부사 (adverbs) flexibly. Spanish frequency adverbs go **right after the verb** or **at the start** for emphasis. Don't place between pronoun and verb (always avoid *yo siempre voy*; use *siempre voy* or *voy siempre*).

### 6. "Mediodía" and "medianoche"

Spanish has special words for **noon** (mediodía) and **midnight** (medianoche) — useful for saying "at noon" / "at midnight" without numbers.
